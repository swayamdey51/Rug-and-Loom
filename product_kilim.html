Geometry as Language

Before writing, nomadic tribes of Central Asia used textile geometry as a form of communication. Each diamond, chevron, and stepped border carried meaning — a family's lineage, a bride's prayers, a shepherd's record of the seasons. This kilim draws directly from that tradition, woven by the Rabari community of Kutch whose ancestors migrated from Sindh centuries ago carrying their loom patterns like an oral history in thread.

Unlike pile carpets, a kilim has no knots. The pattern is created entirely by the interlock of warp and weft threads — a process that demands the weaver hold the entire design in memory, row by row, without the aid of a drawn pattern. The geometric precision of this kilim is not drawn; it is calculated and memorised.

The result is a textile that is simultaneously ancient and strikingly modern — a flat-woven surface whose bold geometry translates as naturally into a contemporary interior as it does in a traditional one. 1

Wool Preparation

Local Kachchi sheep wool is hand-washed in the Rann's brackish water — a traditional practice Rabari weavers believe conditions the fibre. It is then sun-dried, hand-carded, and hand-spun on a wooden takli (drop spindle) into yarn with a characteristic irregular twist.

2

Natural Dyeing

Each colour is dyed in a separate batch using traditional Kutchi recipes. Deep madder red uses pomegranate rind and alum mordant. Indigo is fermented in clay pots. Turmeric gives the golden ochre. Iron-rich water from old wells produces the charcoal-black.

3

Setting the Warp

Cotton warp threads are set on a horizontal ground loom — the traditional kilim loom of the Rabari community. The loom is pegged directly to the ground and the weaver sits in a pit below it, passing the weft shuttle through at eye level.

4

Interlocking Weft

The pattern is created using the interlocking weft technique: where two colour areas meet, the weft threads of each colour wrap around each other at the boundary, creating a continuous surface with no gaps. Far more technically demanding than the slit-weave technique used in many kilims.

5

Beating the Weft

After each weft pass, the weaver uses a wooden beater to press the thread firmly against the previous row. A consistent, firm beat is what gives this kilim its flat, smooth, durable surface.

6

Finishing & Fringing

The warp ends are twisted and knotted by hand to create the characteristic fringe. The kilim is then hand-washed in cold water and laid flat to dry in the sun. The finished piece is hand-signed by the weaver on the back before leaving the workshop.

The Rabari Weavers of Kutch

The Rabari are a pastoral community of Kutch, Gujarat, whose kilim-weaving tradition stretches back more than 400 years. Originally nomads who moved their flocks across the Rann of Kutch with the seasons, they wove kilims as functional objects — floor coverings, sleeping mats, animal trappings, and dowry gifts. Every piece told a family story.

The 2001 Bhuj earthquake devastated the Rabari weaving community, destroying workshops and looms across the region. The recovery of the craft over the following two decades is largely credited to organisations like the Khamir Craft Resource Centre, which provided infrastructure, training, and market access to displaced weavers.

This kilim was woven by Deviben Rabari, a third-generation weaver from Bhujodi village who learned the craft from her grandmother. Rug & Loom sources exclusively from the Bhujodi Weavers' Cluster and pays above-market rates to ensure the craft remains economically viable for younger generations.

Caring for Your Kilim

Shake & Beat Outdoors

Kilims are best cleaned by taking them outdoors and shaking firmly, then gently beating the back with a soft broom. This removes embedded dust far more effectively than vacuuming. Do this monthly for best results.

Use on Both Sides

Kilims are reversible — flip periodically to distribute wear evenly. Both sides are equally strong and the pattern appears identical in reverse.

Spot Clean with Cold Water

For spills, blot immediately with a clean white cloth. For stains, dab with cold water and a small amount of mild wool detergent, working from the outside of the stain inward. Never rub.

Avoid Folding for Storage

Always roll kilims for storage — never fold. Folding creates permanent crease lines in the flat weave that are very difficult to remove. Roll around an acid-free tube and store in a breathable cotton sleeve.

No Beater Bar Attachments

If vacuuming, use only the suction head — never a beater bar or brush roll. The flat weave of a kilim can be damaged and pulled loose by rotating brush attachments.

Kilim-Specific Notes

Kilims are flat-woven without pile, making them significantly more durable for high-traffic areas than pile carpets of equivalent size. The fringe ends are the most vulnerable part — avoid vacuuming over them directly, and check periodically that the end-knots are secure. If a fringe end comes loose, simply re-knot it by hand.

Wall Hanging Option

This kilim can also be displayed as wall art. Attach a wooden dowel through the top fringe loops and hang using picture wire. This is a traditional Rabari display method and keeps the kilim completely flat. The bold geometric pattern makes an exceptional visual statement at this scale.
