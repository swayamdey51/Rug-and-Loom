A Design Born in a Merchant's Archive

In the 1880s, the Manchester textile firm of Ziegler & Co. commissioned Persian master weavers to redesign traditional Mahal carpets for European drawing rooms. The result — looser floral fields, softened palettes, and generous negative space — became one of the most beloved carpet aesthetics in history. This piece is a direct homage to that tradition, reinterpreted by the weavers of Bhadohi using materials entirely sourced from the Indian subcontinent.

The terracotta and ivory palette of this carpet is drawn from the walls of old havelis in Rajasthan — the sun-bleached ochre of clay bricks, the warmth of aged plaster, the rust of iron-oxide soil after monsoon. These are colours that make a room feel inhabited, lived-in, and deeply at ease.

At 8 × 10 feet and with a pile height of 10mm, this is a carpet designed to anchor an entire room — to be the first thing a guest notices and the last thing they remember. It is the largest and most labour-intensive piece in the New Arrivals collection.

Terracotta tones from pomegranate rind, iron mordant, and red clay. These natural dyes will mellow and deepen with age — the carpet will look better in 20 years than it does today.

Room-Scale Masterwork

At 8 × 10 ft, this carpet contains over 4.6 million individual knots. Maintaining pattern integrity at this scale requires decades of master-level experience.

1

Wool Sourcing & Hand-Spinning

Raw fleece from Ghazni sheep is cleaned and hand-carded in Afghanistan before being shipped to Bhadohi. It is then hand-spun on traditional wooden charkhas, giving the yarn a distinctive irregular texture that holds dye more deeply than machine-spun alternatives.

2

Vegetable Dyeing

The terracotta is achieved through a two-bath process: first pomegranate rind mordant, then red clay suspension. Each batch is dyed separately to create the subtle colour variation characteristic of authentic Ziegler-style rugs.

3

Warping the Loom

Cotton warp threads are stretched on a vertical wooden loom — the same design used for centuries in Persian workshops. The warp is measured and set for exact dimensions; any error at this stage cannot be corrected later.

4

Asymmetric Knotting

The Persian Senneh asymmetric knot is tied individually around each pair of warp threads. Two weavers work side by side across the 8-foot width, each responsible for their half of the design, requiring constant coordination.

5

Washing & Stretching

The finished carpet is hand-washed in cold water with a mild lanolin solution, then stretched to exact dimensions on a wooden frame and sun-dried for five days. This step also softens the carpet to its final, supple texture.

6

Hand Shearing & Finishing

The pile is hand-sheared to a consistent 10mm height using traditional scissors. Edges are overcast in matching wool to prevent fraying. A final inspection checks every square inch for knot consistency before the carpet is approved for sale.

The Weavers of Bhadohi

Bhadohi, in eastern Uttar Pradesh, is known as the "Carpet City of India" — a region with over 600 years of continuous weaving history. The town and its surrounding villages are home to more than 500,000 people engaged in the carpet trade, from wool traders to master weavers to dye specialists.

The Ziegler Terracotta Classic was woven by the Ansari Weaving House — a family operation now in its fifth generation, founded in 1921 by Ustad Bashir Ahmad Ansari. The current lead weaver, his great-great-grandson Farrukh Ansari, trained under his father from age 10 and received the Shilp Guru award from the Ministry of Textiles in 2019.

Rug & Loom's partnership with the Ansari Weaving House began in 2021. Under our agreement, 55% of the final sale price goes directly to the weaving household — and we provide advance payment before weaving begins, so artisans are never dependent on a sale to fund their work.

Caring for Your Ziegler

Vacuum Regularly

Vacuum weekly in the direction of the pile without a beater bar. The 10mm high pile traps dust more readily than low-pile rugs — regular vacuuming is essential.

Rotate Every 6 Months

Rotate 180° every 6 months to distribute foot traffic evenly and prevent uneven pile compression in high-use areas.

Use a Quality Rug Pad

A breathable felt and rubber rug pad is essential for a carpet of this weight and size. It prevents floor abrasion, reduces slippage, and extends the life of the carpet backing.

Avoid Prolonged Sunlight

While vegetable dyes are stable, prolonged direct sunlight over years will cause gradual fading of the terracotta tones. Use UV-filtering glass or curtains near large windows.

Never Machine Wash

Machine washing will destroy the knot structure and permanently distort the pile. Professional hand-washing every 3–5 years is recommended.

High-Pile Specific Care

New high-pile carpets will shed fine fibres for the first 2–3 months of use. This is completely normal and not a sign of quality loss — it is simply loose fibres from the weaving process working their way free. Vacuuming frequently during this period will resolve it quickly.

Pile Restoration Tip

If the pile becomes flattened in high-traffic areas, lightly mist with cold water and use a soft-bristle brush to gently lift the fibres back to their original direction. Allow to dry naturally — never use heat.
